SupportStatement: “this gadget is like many other healthcare products available for purchase: the marketing has gotten way ahead of the science.”
-Robert H. Shmerling, MD

There is the potential for individuals to delay or avoid seeking proper medical treatment. For example, if someone is led to believe that a certain pseudoscientific practice or product can cure their illness, they may delay seeking medical attention from a qualified healthcare professional.

This delay could result in the condition worsening or becoming more serious, leading to negative health outcomes.

Advertising these products can have serious effects on peoples health and trust in progress in science. Products that are falsely promoted for being supported by science may result in impeding on scientific progress in legitimate research.

Pseudoscience related advertisements have been around much longer than social media and online advertising have been. Popular pseudoscience advertising included newspaper advertising blood letting, a process involving attaching leeches to the skin. This process was widely believed to be a trusted treatment to rid ‘poisons’ in the body.

As of today we have advanced research and treatments for the illnesses of the past, yet certain communities today still believe that old treatments are the best option. Many of these individuals have turned to anti-vaccine beliefs and natural methods of treatment, usually without leeches.

Social media has given people the ability to share their opinions online and have conversations with people all around the world. Social media has also led to misinformation about vaccines being the cause of autism and having more negative effects than positives although they have led to re-emergence of preventable diseases such as measles and mumps.

Another potential harm is the financial cost to individuals who purchase pseudoscientific products or services that are marketed as "miracle cures" or "quick fixes" for various ailments.

These products may be expensive and have no real therapeutic value, leading to a waste of resources that could have been spent on evidence-based treatments or therapies.

Products like the one above have become popular advertisements on Instagram. Through the use of algorithms, products can be better targeted towards possible buyers. This also means that possible scam products can be better at targeting people based on their age, online activity, and interests/needs.

Robert H. Shmerling, MD from Harvard has made a short article and statement that best sums up the idea of anti-stress technology:

These high-tech devices are not required in ones life. “While it would be great if such a gadget could safely reduce symptoms and complications of chronic stress, without solid evidence it's impossible to know whether any of the claims made by the makers of Apollo Neuro are true”.

It is also worth noting that advertising pseudoscientific products can contribute to the spread of misinformation and a lack of critical thinking among the general public. This can lead to a mistrust of legitimate scientific practices and research, and ultimately hinder the progress of scientific advancement.
While it is important to promote freedom of speech and allow for different viewpoints to be expressed, it is equally important to prioritize the health and well-being of individuals and ensure that advertising practices are ethical and transparent.
