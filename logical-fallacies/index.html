Logical_Fallacies: Refers to the many forms of “error in reasoning” through appeals to authority, us vs. them advertising, scare tactics, false dilemmas, and hasty generalizations.

One common fallacy used in pseudoscientific advertisements is the appeal to authority. Advertisers may use celebrities, doctors, or other authority figures to lend credibility to their claims, even if those claims lack scientific evidence.

In the past it was theoretical dentists and doctors or famous actors used to appeal to buyer. With social media, not only is it licensed professionals and celebrities but also influencers and sponsored testimonials.

It is important to be wary of these appeals to authority and to always look for scientific evidence to support claims made in advertisements.

Another form of logical fallacy comes in the form of a bandwagon. These ads may feed into peoples need to be apart of the newest trend.

Detox teas and fad diets may promote themselves as being “popular”, that “everyone” is trying it or that it has seen amazing results with xxxx number of people.

Facebook in the past had a ‘pseudoscience and conspiracy theory targeting’ section for those running advertisements on their site. This allowed for many people to receive targeted ads that heavily featured logical fallacies.
